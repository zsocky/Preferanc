History

Despite having a French name and a number of French terms, the game has never really taken off in France. While Depaulis has discovered references as early as 1801 in Bohemia and notes that it may have even been known in Russia before 1800, a game by this name was already mentioned as being popular in Vienna in 1803. Préférence quickly gained popularity in Imperial Russia as well, despite the fact that the earliest description is from an 1829 Austrian game anthology. The Russian Preferans' suit order evolved into the modern Contract Bridge suit order through Vint. Dice games, chess, backgammon, tarot games, Préférence, and gambling card games were among the games played by the Christian population of the Ottoman Empire (Greeks, Macedonians, Vlachs and Serbs, Slavs) as of 1846, according to a German encyclopedia.

Preférence appears to have been influenced by Ombre and Boston, though it also superficially resembles other Central European games like Skat and Mariá as a three-player game with 10-card hands and a 2-card talon. Although it shares many similarities with the simpler French four-player game Boston de Fontainebleau, which appears to be the source of the French terminology, it has the same basic structure as Ombre. According to McLeod and Geiser, Préférence belongs to the Rams family of card games, which are distinguished by the option for players to refuse a deal if they feel their hand is insufficient to win a trick or the minimum number of tricks required.

Préférence gets its name from the order in which preferred suits are ranked for use in bidding, which was a novel feature at the time of its introduction. If both parties are still represented after a mode of play has been declared, any player may withdraw and only the remaining players play. This feature is similar to those found in gambling games like Tippen or Loo. The game is also referred to in Austria as Pudeln, Polackeln, or Polacheln; the last two names imply a connection to Poland because Polacken is the Austro-Bavarian word for "Poles," which is related to the Italian word polacco. Additionally, according to Geiser, the game is known as Wallachen or Walachen in Bavaria, suggesting a connection to modern-day Romania's Wallachia. Bolachen, a name that is phonetically similar to the Austro-Bavarian Polacheln, is a condensed version of Wallachen. The game is known in Vienna as Preferl, a playful moniker.
